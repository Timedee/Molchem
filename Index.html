Mole Conversions (given in Part B)

COMM

Starting from MOLES

Find mass: m = n × MM
Find particles: # = n × 6.02×10²³

Starting from MASS

Step 1: n = m ÷ MM    → Step 2: mole ratio → Step 3: answer
Ex: 3.4g N₂ → find mass NH₃ (N₂+3H₂→2NH₃)
n(N₂) = 3.4÷28.0 = 0.121 mol
n(NH₃) = 0.121 × (2÷1) = 0.242 mol
m(NH₃) = 0.242 × 17.0 = 4.1 g

Starting from PARTICLES

Ex: 3.01×10²³ molecules → find mass of H₂O
n = 3.01×10²³ ÷ 6.02×10²³ = 0.500 mol
m = 0.500 × 18.0 = 9.0 g

% Composition

MC + COMM
% element = (mass of element in formula / total molar mass) × 100%
Ex: Mg(OH)₂ — MM = 24.3 + 16.0×2 + 1.0×2 = 58.3 g/mol
% Mg = 24.3÷58.3 × 100 = 41.7%
% O = 32.0÷58.3 × 100 = 54.9%
% H = 2.0÷58.3 × 100 = 3.46%
✓ Check: 41.7 + 54.9 + 3.46 ≈ 100%
⚠️ To find mass of element in a sample: mass × (element MM / formula MM)

Empirical & Molecular Formulas

MC + COMM

Empirical Formula — 4 Steps

1
% → assume 100g → you now have grams of each element
2
g ÷ MM → moles of each element
3
÷ by smallest moles → ratio
4
× multiplier if needed to get whole numbers (×2 if .5, ×3 if .33)
Ex: 52.2%C, 13.0%H, 34.8%O
C: 52.2÷12.0=4.35   H: 13.0÷1.0=13.0   O: 34.8÷16.0=2.175
÷2.175: C=2, H=6, O=1 → EF = C₂H₆O

Molecular Formula

a = Molecular Mass ÷ EF mass     MF = EF × a
EF = CH (mass=13u), Mol. mass=78u → a=6 → MF = C₆H₆

Limiting Reagent — Part C Will Have This

★ HIGH VALUE
⭐ The limiting reagent (LR) is the reactant that runs out first and stops the reaction.
1
Balance the equation
2
Convert all reactant masses → moles (n = m ÷ MM)
3
Divide each reactant's moles by its coefficient
4
Smallest result = Limiting Reagent
5
Use LR moles × (coeff product ÷ coeff LR) → moles of product
6
m = n × MM → mass of product
Na₂CO₃ + CaCl₂ → CaCO₃ + 2NaCl
34.7g Na₂CO₃: n=34.7÷106.0=0.327mol → ÷1 = 0.327
41.2g CaCl₂: n=41.2÷111.0=0.371mol → ÷1 = 0.371
LR = Na₂CO₃ (0.327 is smaller)
n(NaCl) = 0.327 × (2÷1) = 0.654 mol
m(NaCl) = 0.654 × 58.5 = 38.3 g
2Al(OH)₃ + 3H₂SO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 6H₂O
21.0g Al(OH)₃: n=0.269mol → ÷2 = 0.1346
40.7g H₂SO₄: n=0.415mol → ÷3 = 0.1383
LR = Al(OH)₃ (0.1346 < 0.1383)
n(H₂O) = 0.269 × (6÷2) = 0.807 mol → m = 0.807×18.0 = 14.5 g

Hydrate Lab Problem — Part C

Your teacher said: "a problem very similar to your hydrate lab — calculate the formula of a hydrate being given data like you collected." This is the exact method.

★ Method: Finding x in Salt·xH₂O

STEP BY STEP
A hydrate heats → loses water → anhydrous salt remains.
You get: mass of hydrate and mass of anhydrous salt (or crucible masses to calculate it).
1
Find mass of water lost
m(H₂O) = m(hydrate) − m(anhydrous salt)
2
Find moles of anhydrous salt
n(salt) = m(salt) ÷ MM(salt)
3
Find moles of water
n(H₂O) = m(H₂O) ÷ 18.0
4
Find ratio x
x = n(H₂O) ÷ n(salt) → round to nearest whole number
5
Write the formula: Salt·xH₂O
